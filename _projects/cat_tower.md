<h2>Motivation and Initial Design</h2>

<div class="blog_image right small">
    <img src="/img/cat_tower/maya_in_old_perch.jpg">
    <p>1 of our 2 cats, Maya, couldn't even lay her head down on the perch of our old cat tower.</p>
</div>

During the my summer break of 2026, I was requested by my family to refurbish the "cat palace" that we only bought 1 year ago for our two kittens. To my surprise, I came back to stripped rope, a snapped platform and a wobble that competed with my $11 IKEA lamp. So I figured: Why not just... design one from scratch? That way, we could make it bigger, sturdier, more aesthetically pleasing, and wild enough to satisfy my hidden woodworking cravings.

My initial design was based around a simple concept: <b>utilizing the existing corner of the room in a more efficient way</b>. There was room to widen the tower two-fold, but we wanted to preserve the view out of the vertical window at the far side of the room. We wanted at least 1 cubby, and high perches suitable for both lounging under the sun and for batting at each other. The cats loved racing up and down the tower, so the design needed to provide several accessible routes to the top. I conferred with my family and studied how our cats interacted with the old tower, and came up with a unique design that tackled all of these requirements.

<h2>Okay... Now How To Actually Start?</h2>

With the design of the tower locked down, material and tool selection was the next step. As this was our first major woodworking project and we had no prior tools aside from a drill, I needed to ensure that the design was feasible with as few power tools as possible. <mark>Pine construction lumber</mark> was the obvious choice for the 2x4s and 2x6s that form the frame, and we opted for <mark>1/2" plywood</mark> instead of the much cheaper OSB to avoid the headache of splintering. We tried 1/8" hardboard for the flexible cubby and perch walls, but after it snapped during a bend test, we switched to <mark>1/8" utility panel</mark> (which still was unfortunately prone to cracking, but it was good enough). Then, for the covering, we'd use a <mark>self-adhesive cat carpet</mark>, a <mark>cork board</mark> for climbing, and <mark>sisal rope</mark> for scratching. Finally, we bought a <mark>handheld circular saw</mark> for cutting and a <mark>random orbital sander</mark> for smoothing the faces and edges.

<h2>Cutting, Shaping and Staining</h2>

<div class="blog_image right small">
    <img src="/img/cat_tower/cutting_lumber.jpg">
    <p>The cut construction lumber.</p>
</div>

Then the real work began. We cut the 2x2s, 2x4s, and 2x6s to size, carefully measuring lengths and printing to-scale diagrams of angles to ensure accuracy. 
<br><br><br><br><br><br><br><br><br>

<div class="blog_image left">
    <img src="/img/cat_tower/plywood_diagrams.jpg">
    <p>These 2 printed diagrams took outlines directly from the CAD model and were arranged in a way to avoid wasted space or having to buy a 3rd plywood sheet.</p>
</div>

The CAD model was invaluable in aligning all of the cutouts on the 2 plywood sheets on 2 diagrams so I made use of them efficiently. 

<br><br><br><br><br><br><br><br><br><br><br><br><br>

The plywood cutting was the most tedious part of the project. Without a table saw, I had to work on the ground and follow long, straight lines carefully with only the in-built laser guide on the circular saw. The inner corner cuts required me to make <b>plunge cuts</b> and finish with a hand saw to avoid having to overshoot the cut.

<h2>Creating the Cubbies</h2>

The cubbies turned out to be one of the most challenging parts of the project. The walls were made from 1/8" utility panel, which was prone to cracking, even after a night of pre-bending. I worked with my stepdad to glue and staple the walls to the cubby "skeleton" (made out of the plywood cutouts and 2x2s), and then with my mom to wrap it in carpet.

<h2>Assembly!</h2>

At this point, I had spent the entire time thinking that assembly would be the satisfyingly simple part of the project, just like building IKEA furniture. Instead, it felt like piecing together a 3D puzzle with crossing dependencies, imperfect cuts and measurements, and all with no linear path forward. I realized how crucial it was to design the tower with ease of assembly in mind. But thanks to my caution and precision cutting the components, the assembly process was for the most part smooth.
